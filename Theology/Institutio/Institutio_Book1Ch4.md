In **Book 2, Chapter 4** of the *Institutes*, Calvin tackles a very difficult and provocative question: **If humans are "slaves to sin" (as he argued in the previous chapters), how does God interact with the human will during sinful acts?**

The chapter is titled: *"How God Works in the Hearts of Men."* Having established that the human will is "bound" by sin, Calvin now explains the mechanics of how God, the Devil, and Man all play a role in human actions without making God the author of evil.

As we’ve done before, I’ll break this down from a beginner level, focusing on the "threefold action" Calvin describes.

---

### 1. The Three Actors in One Event

Calvin’s main point is that when a sinful act occurs, there are often **three different wills** at work simultaneously. He uses the biblical story of **Job** as his primary example. When the Sabeans stole Job's cattle and killed his servants:

1. **Satan** wanted to destroy Job’s faith.
2. **The Sabeans** wanted to get rich by stealing.
3. **God** intended to test Job’s patience and refine his soul.

Calvin argues that God is not "onlooker" but is sovereignly using the wicked desires of the other two to accomplish His holy purpose.

### 2. How Satan Works in the Wicked

Calvin explains that Satan is the "prince of this world," but he is not an independent power.

* **The "Blinding" of the Mind:** Satan works within the wicked by blinding their understanding and hardening their hearts.
* **God’s Permission:** However, Satan can only do what God allows. Calvin calls Satan God's "executioner." God uses Satan’s malice to punish the wicked or discipline the godly.

### 3. How God Works in the Hearts of Men

This is the most controversial part of the chapter. Calvin argues that God doesn't just "permit" things to happen; He actively directs them.

* **Hardening the Heart:** Calvin addresses the Bible verses that say "God hardened Pharaoh's heart." He argues that this is more than just God "letting it happen." God withdraws His light and grace, leaving the person to their own corruption, and He directs their existing malice toward a specific end.
* **Directing the Will:** God can turn the hearts of men (even kings) wherever He pleases to fulfill His plans, yet He does this without forcing them to do anything they don't already *want* to do.

### 4. Why God is Not "The Author of Sin"

Calvin is very careful to distinguish between the **act** and the **intent**.

* **Man's Intent is Evil:** The Sabeans (and Satan) act out of pure malice and greed. Therefore, they are guilty.
* **God's Intent is Good:** God uses the *same event* for a righteous and holy purpose.
* **The Analogy of the Sun:** Calvin uses a famous analogy: The sun’s heat makes a dead body smell bad, but the sun itself is not "stinky." The "stink" comes from the corruption already inside the body. Similarly, God’s power moves all things, but the "stink" of sin comes from the corruption in the human heart, not from God.

---

### Summary Table: The Threefold Causality

| Actor | Motive/Intent | Moral Standing |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **God** | Justice, Discipline, or Glory. | **Holy and Righteous.** |
| **Satan** | Malice, Rebellion, and Destruction. | **Wicked and Condemned.** |
| **Man** | Greed, Lust, or Pride. | **Guilty and Responsible.** |

### Key Takeaway for the Beginner

Calvin wants you to see that **God is in total control**, even of the actions of the wicked and the Devil. This is meant to give the believer great comfort: even when evil people or spiritual forces attack you, they are ultimately "on a leash" held by your Heavenly Father, who is using the event for your ultimate good.
