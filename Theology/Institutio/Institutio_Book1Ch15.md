In **Book 1, Chapter 15** of the *Institutes of the Christian Religion*, John Calvin shifts his focus from the nature of God to the nature of humanity as originally created. The chapter is titled: *"Man as He Was Created, Wherein the Faculties of the Soul, the Image of God, Free Will, and the Original Integrity of Our Nature are Discussed."*

Since you are interested in a specific chapter, would you like me to provide a breakdown starting from a beginner level (explaining the basic terms), or should I dive straight into the advanced theological implications?

For now, here is a high-level summary of the key themes in Chapter 15:

### 1. The Knowledge of Self and God

Calvin begins by stating that we cannot truly know God without also understanding our own nature. To appreciate the depth of our current fallen state, we must first understand the "integrity" and "perfection" of how God originally designed us. He warns against focusing only on our current misery, as we must first recognize the "nobility" of our original creation.

### 2. The Duality of Man: Body and Soul

Calvin emphasizes that a human being consists of two parts: **body** and **soul**.

* He argues for the **immortality of the soul**, asserting that it is a distinct, non-material essence created by God.
* The soul is the primary seat of the "Image of God." While the body reflects God's glory in a secondary way, it is the soul that truly bears the divine likeness.

### 3. The Image of God (*Imago Dei*)

Calvin defines the "Image of God" not as a physical appearance, but as a spiritual and intellectual likeness.

* **Original State:** Before the Fall, the image of God was manifested in "light of intellect, rectitude of heart, and the soundness of every part."
* **Knowledge, Righteousness, and Holiness:** Drawing from the New Testament (specifically Colossians 3:10 and Ephesians 4:24), Calvin argues that the image consists of true knowledge, righteousness, and holiness.
* **Current State:** He notes that while this image was not totally annihilated by sin, it was "viciously corrupted" and "frightfully deformed."

### 4. The Two Faculties of the Soul

Calvin simplifies the complexities of the human psyche into two primary faculties:

* **The Understanding (Intellect):** Its job is to distinguish between objects, to discern what is good or evil, and to perceive what is true or false. It is the "guide and governor" of the soul.
* **The Will:** Its job is to choose and follow what the understanding identifies as good, and to reject what it identifies as evil.

### 5. Free Will in the State of Innocence

This is a crucial theological point for Calvin. He argues that in the original state of creation (before Adam sinned):

* **Man had Free Will:** Adam had the power and "uprightness" to choose good and obtain eternal life if he so willed. His will was capable of following his reason.
* **The Fall:** Adam fell because he *willed* to fall. Calvin insists that God is not the author of sin; rather, Adam was given a choice and, though he was capable of standing, he chose to disobey.
* **Contrast with Today:** Calvin makes it clear that while Adam had this "original integrity," the human will is now so corrupted by sin that we no longer possess that same freedom to choose the spiritual good without God's transforming grace.

To understand John Calvin’s view on the **Faculties of the Soul** in *Institutes* 1.15, it helps to think of the soul as a government or a high-functioning machine designed by God.

Since you asked to start from a beginner level, let’s look at why Calvin even brings this up. He wants to show that when God created us, we weren't just "flesh and blood"—we were equipped with a spiritual "command center" that functioned perfectly.

## 1. The Two-Part Map of the Soul

Calvin simplifies the human experience into two main powers or "faculties." He moves away from the complex philosophical maps of the Greeks (like Aristotle) and focuses on how these two parts interact:

### **A. The Understanding (The "Pilot" or "Eyes")**

The Understanding is the intellectual part of the soul. Its primary job is to **see, evaluate, and judge**.

* **Its Function:** It distinguishes between true and false, and between what is good and what is evil.
* **Its Role:** Calvin calls it the "guide and governor" of the soul. In Adam's original state, the Understanding was filled with light. It knew God clearly and understood the moral law perfectly.

### **B. The Will (The "Engine" or "Feet")**

The Will is the desiring part of the soul. Once the Understanding points at something and says, "That is good," the Will is supposed to **choose and move toward it**.

* **Its Function:** It chooses, desires, and pursues.
* **Its Role:** It is the servant of the Understanding. It doesn't "think"; it just acts based on what the Understanding presents to it.

---

## 2. How it Worked "Out of the Box" (Original Integrity)

Calvin argues that in our original "factory setting" (Adam before the Fall), these two faculties worked in perfect harmony:

1. **The Understanding** saw God and His commands as the highest Good.
2. **The Will** followed that lead with joy and total obedience.

There was no "internal war." There was no desire to do wrong because the Understanding knew that wrong was destructive, and the Will had no desire to rebel against what was true.

---

## 3. The "Choice" (Free Will in Chapter 15)

This is where Calvin addresses the controversial topic of "Free Will." He explains that in this original state, Adam’s Will was **truly free**.

* **Ability to Stand:** Adam had the "uprightness" and the power to choose to remain faithful to God.
* **Possibility of Falling:** While Adam was capable of choosing good, he was not *incapable* of choosing evil. His will was flexible.

> **Calvin’s Point:** If Adam fell, it wasn't because God made a "broken" machine. It was because Adam, of his own accord, allowed his Will to turn away from the light of his Understanding.

---

## 4. Why This Matters for Us Today

Calvin explains these "Faculties" to show us what we have **lost**.

* **The Understanding is now blind:** We often mistake evil for good or lies for truth.
* **The Will is now shackled:** Even when we know something is right, our Will often lacks the power or desire to do it.

By understanding that the soul was designed to have a "perfect Pilot" (Understanding) and a "perfect Engine" (Will), we can see more clearly how much we need the Holy Spirit to **renew our minds** and **change our hearts**.
