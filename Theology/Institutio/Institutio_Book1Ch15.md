In **Book 1, Chapter 15** of the *Institutes of the Christian Religion*, John Calvin shifts his focus from the nature of God to the nature of humanity as originally created. The chapter is titled: *"Man as He Was Created, Wherein the Faculties of the Soul, the Image of God, Free Will, and the Original Integrity of Our Nature are Discussed."*

For now, here is a high-level summary of the key themes in Chapter 15:

### 1. The Knowledge of Self and God

Calvin begins by stating that we cannot truly know God without also understanding our own nature. To appreciate the depth of our current fallen state, we must first understand the "integrity" and "perfection" of how God originally designed us. He warns against focusing only on our current misery, as we must first recognize the "nobility" of our original creation.

### 2. The Duality of Man: Body and Soul

Calvin emphasizes that a human being consists of two parts: **body** and **soul**.

* He argues for the **immortality of the soul**, asserting that it is a distinct, non-material essence created by God.
* The soul is the primary seat of the "Image of God." While the body reflects God's glory in a secondary way, it is the soul that truly bears the divine likeness.

### 3. The Image of God (*Imago Dei*)

Calvin defines the "Image of God" not as a physical appearance, but as a spiritual and intellectual likeness.

* **Original State:** Before the Fall, the image of God was manifested in "light of intellect, rectitude of heart, and the soundness of every part."
* **Knowledge, Righteousness, and Holiness:** Drawing from the New Testament (specifically Colossians 3:10 and Ephesians 4:24), Calvin argues that the image consists of true knowledge, righteousness, and holiness.
* **Current State:** He notes that while this image was not totally annihilated by sin, it was "viciously corrupted" and "frightfully deformed."

### 4. The Two Faculties of the Soul

Calvin simplifies the complexities of the human psyche into two primary faculties:

* **The Understanding (Intellect):** Its job is to distinguish between objects, to discern what is good or evil, and to perceive what is true or false. It is the "guide and governor" of the soul.
* **The Will:** Its job is to choose and follow what the understanding identifies as good, and to reject what it identifies as evil.

### 5. Free Will in the State of Innocence

This is a crucial theological point for Calvin. He argues that in the original state of creation (before Adam sinned):

* **Man had Free Will:** Adam had the power and "uprightness" to choose good and obtain eternal life if he so willed. His will was capable of following his reason.
* **The Fall:** Adam fell because he *willed* to fall. Calvin insists that God is not the author of sin; rather, Adam was given a choice and, though he was capable of standing, he chose to disobey.
* **Contrast with Today:** Calvin makes it clear that while Adam had this "original integrity," the human will is now so corrupted by sin that we no longer possess that same freedom to choose the spiritual good without God's transforming grace.
